Bacterial diversity in soils and rhizospheres of Admiralty Bay, King George Island, Antarctica.

Lia Cardoso Rocha Saraiva Teixeira. Advisors: Alexandre Soares Rosado e Raquel Silva Peixoto

Abstract

The knowledge about the effects that is directly influence microbial diversity in different environments is still a field in expansion. If we consider extreme environments, this discussion may be even crucial, taking into account global changes that can strongly influence these habitats. In order to evaluate the bacterial communities we collected Deschampsia Antarctica and Colobanthus quitensis rhizospheres and bulk soils from six different sampling sites at the Admiralty Bay, Maritime Antarctic. The DNA of the bacterial community associated with the rhizospheres was evaluated using a 16S rRNA multiplex 454 pyrosequencing approach. This analysis revealed similar patterns of bacterial diversity between the two plant species. Firmicutes was the most abundant phylum in almost all the analyzed samples, followed by Actinobacteria and Proteobacteria. Microarrays and qPCR were used to compare bacterial communities from different soils and adjacent rhizospheres. The results indicated a rhizosphere effect in 4 of the analyzed sites, separating specific bacterial profiles in bulk soils or rhizopsheres. The phyla Acidobacteria was positively influenced by non-ornithogenic bulk soil and negatively influenced by rhizosphere effect. On the other hand, Actinobacteria was positively influenced by rhizospheres of both plant species. Comparing the two plant species, the phylum Firmicutes was positively influenced by D. antactica rhizosphere in two sampling sites. The Firmicutes phylum was also positively correlated to the nitrogen concentrations in the soil. The bacterial community could be manly driven by the bulk soil conditions, but the rhizospheres could shape this already selected community in Antarctica.
